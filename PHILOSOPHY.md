## Why This License Exists

The licenses that protect software freedom were written
for a different world.

The GPL was written when the enemy was proprietary
lock-in. The MIT license was written when the goal was
maximum adoption. The BSL was written when the threat
was platform competition. All were right for their moment.

This is a different moment.

Platforms extract value from creators at unprecedented
scale. Governments surveil with capabilities previous
generations couldn't imagine. AI tools of extraordinary
power assist human creativity — and institutions seek
to claim ownership of what those humans made. The commons
is being enclosed again, by different means but the
same intent.

The MPLS License is written for this moment.

It stands in a lineage:

    GNU  — GNU's Not Unix         (1983, software freedom)
    GIMP — GNU Image Manipulation (1995, creative tools)
    MPLS — MPLS Principled Libre  (2025, the agentic era)

Forty years of that tradition. Each one a tool built on
values, named with intention, owned by the community
that uses it. Same spirit. New threats. New instrument.

## What This License Believes

**People first.**

Always. When the interests of institutions conflict with
the interests of people, people win. This is not balanced
against business needs. It is the tiebreaker.

**Creators own what they build.**

If you conceived it, directed it, and shaped it —
you made it. What you made is yours.

This includes work created with AI assistance or any
other tool. The idea was yours. The direction was yours.
The judgment of what to keep and what to discard was
yours. That is authorship. That is ownership.

The tool does not own the work.
The platform does not own the work.
The AI does not own the work.
You do.

No institution — corporate, governmental, or otherwise —
may claim ownership of your creative work on the basis
that a tool assisted in its creation. A paintbrush
doesn't own the painting. A compiler doesn't own the
program. A powerful assistant doesn't own the idea it
helped realize.

If you made it, it is yours.

**Safety is the default, not the option.**

Privacy from governments. Privacy from corporations.
Privacy from bad actors. Protection from systems built
to exploit. These are not features to be enabled.
They are the ground state.

**Freedom and protection belong together.**

You can have both. This license proves it.

The creativity to build anything.
The openness for everyone to benefit.
The protection this moment requires.

## The Threshold — Why $1M

Under $1M annual revenue and under 100K users, Shrike
is free with no conditions beyond attribution.

At $1M you have found something that works. The commons
helped you get there — open tools, shared knowledge,
the work of people who built before you. $1M is the
point where the commons asks something back. Not before.
Not when you are still building, struggling, figuring
it out.

$1M is a clear number. Everyone knows what it means.
It does not require a finance team to calculate.
It is generous to those who are growing and fair to
those who have grown.

## Creator Rights — The AI Question

The question of authorship in an era of powerful AI
tools is being contested in courts, copyright offices,
and boardrooms. The MPLS License takes a clear position:

The prompt is the creative act.
The idea is the work.
The AI is the tool.

This has always been true of tools. The printing press
did not own the books it printed. The camera did not
own the photographs it captured. The word processor
did not own the novels it formatted.

A powerful tool does not change the nature of authorship.
It changes the scale and speed of what one person can
create. The person who conceived, directed, and shaped
the work is its author. Full stop.

No platform may extract that authorship.
No institution may claim it.
No license condition imposed by a tool's creator may
reach into what you made with that tool.

What you made is yours.

## The Prohibited Uses — The Reasoning

These are not restrictions born from legal caution.
They are restrictions born from values.

**Surveillance** destroys the conditions for free thought,
free association, and free society. A compiler that makes
surveillance easier is a compiler working against the
people who use computers. Shrike refuses that role.

**Weapons** cause death. Software that guides weapons,
targets weapons, or operates weapons contributes to that
death. The people who wrote this code did not write it
so that people could be killed with it. No amount of
money changes that.

**Oppression** uses the power of the state against the
people the state is supposed to serve. Software that
makes oppression more efficient is software that has
chosen a side. The MPLS License has chosen the other
side. Clearly and permanently.

**Manipulation** at scale is a form of violence against
human autonomy. Systems designed to radicalize, deceive,
or psychologically exploit people cause real harm to
real people. Shrike will not be part of that architecture.

**AI training extraction** is the enclosure of the
commons by new means. When the work of individual
creators is harvested without permission to build
commercial systems that then compete with those creators,
something has been taken without consent. The MPLS
License names this clearly and prohibits it.

**Secret government compliance** makes the people who
use software unknowing participants in their own
surveillance or compromise. The only honest response
to a secret order that harms users is disclosure or
refusal. The MPLS License requires one or the other.

## On Enforceability

We are not naive.

We know that determined bad actors ignore license terms.
We know that enforcement across jurisdictions is
imperfect. We know that powerful institutions have
powerful lawyers.

We write these restrictions anyway because:

**Code should stand for something.**

The people who build with this software should know
what it stands for. The people harmed by surveillance,
weapons, and manipulation deserve to know that at least
some software refused.

When the first public release happened, the values were
already in the repository. The mission was committed
before the code could run. The license existed before
the language was useful to anyone.

That sequence is the proof. Not of enforceability —
of intent. Immutable, timestamped, public.

Nobody can say the values were added later.
Nobody can say they were performative.
The git history tells the truth.

## The Lineage

Richard Stallman wrote the GPL manifesto before GNU
was finished. Linus Torvalds posted his note before
Linux could do much. The Free Software Definition was
written before most of the software it protected existed.

The values came first. The software grew to fill them.

The free software movement gave us the right to see
the code. The MPLS License gives us the right to
control what it does and who benefits from it.

We stand on that foundation.
We build forward from it.
We answer the threats this moment presents that
they could not have anticipated.
